About

From Medicine
to Psychology

A story, not a résumé.

After spending years preparing for NEET, I chose to pivot toward psychology — not because it was a backup plan, but because I became increasingly fascinated by the human mind and behavior. The question that wouldn't leave me alone was not how the body works, but why we think, feel, and act the way we do.

I am currently pursuing a B.Sc. in Psychology and hope to specialize in Cognitive Neuroscience in the future. My interest lies at the intersection of science and meaning — in the strange gap between what the brain does and what it feels like to be the brain doing it.

This website, Beautifully Unwell, is my attempt to document that inquiry publicly — through essays, poems, research notes, and video essays. It is a lifelong project.

"Maybe this is what it means to be awake: to feel everything too much and still choose to stay curious."

Consciousness and Predictive Processing

An exploration of how the brain constructs reality through prediction rather than passive perception.

For much of human history, perception was understood as a passive process in which the brain simply received information from the external world. Yet, the emerging framework of predictive processing suggests something far more intriguing: the brain is not merely a recorder of reality but an active constructor of it.

Human beings appear to be prediction machines. Rather than waiting for sensory information to arrive and then interpreting it, the brain continuously generates models about the world and compares these expectations with incoming signals. What we experience as reality is therefore not a direct copy of the external environment, but the brain's best and most useful guess about it. Learning occurs through prediction errors — the discrepancies between expectation and experience — which force these internal models to be revised.

This perspective extends beyond perception. Emotions, beliefs, memories, and even the sense of self may be understood as predictions constructed across different levels of the mind. Consciousness itself can be viewed not as a detached observer, but as an evolving model through which the brain makes sense of its own activity and its relationship with the world.

Metacognition adds another layer to this process. Humans possess the remarkable ability to examine their own thoughts, question their interpretations, and deliberately revise the predictions through which they understand themselves and others. Cognitive reappraisal, reflection, and self-questioning may therefore represent higher-order forms of prediction updating rather than entirely separate psychological processes.

From this perspective, much of human experience emerges from the continual interaction between expectation and error, stability and revision, prediction and surprise. To understand the mind, then, may not require asking how the brain passively perceives reality, but how it actively constructs it.

Footnote

If perception, emotion, and thought are shaped by predictions, then many of our fears, assumptions, and biases may also be inherited or repeatedly reinforced predictions rather than objective truths. Among these, the negativity bias appears especially significant, as the brain often prioritizes potential threats over neutral or positive information. A continuing challenge of metacognition is therefore learning to recognize these deeply ingrained predictions and becoming capable of updating them when they no longer reflect reality.

Cognitive Reappraisal and Error Correction

Revising the mind's predictions.

Human experience is shaped not only by events themselves, but by the meanings we assign to them. Between what happens and how we feel lies a process of interpretation. Cognitive appraisal refers to these subjective interpretations through which individuals make sense of the world around them. In many ways, emotions arise not directly from events, but from the significance the mind attributes to those events.

Cognitive reappraisal represents the ability to revisit those initial interpretations and consider alternative perspectives. Rather than reacting automatically, individuals can examine their assumptions, reflect on different possibilities, and revise their understanding of a situation. In doing so, emotions themselves may change, not because reality has changed, but because the meaning attached to it has.

From the perspective of predictive processing, this process resembles a form of error correction. The brain continuously generates predictions about the world, ourselves, and other people. When experience conflicts with these expectations, prediction errors emerge. Metacognition — the ability to think about one's own thinking — allows these mismatches to become conscious. Cognitive reappraisal can then serve as a deliberate mechanism for updating the beliefs and interpretations that gave rise to the emotional response.

Thus, emotional regulation may not simply involve controlling feelings, but revising the predictions and meanings from which those feelings emerge. Learning, adaptation, and psychological growth may all depend upon this continual process of prediction, reflection, and correction.

Footnote

Negative interpretations often feel self-evident, yet they may represent deeply learned predictions rather than objective truths. Through metacognition and cognitive reappraisal, individuals become capable of questioning these assumptions and gradually updating them. In this sense, growth is not the absence of error, but the willingness to correct it.
